The Twelve Olympians

The Pantheon

Lords and ladies of Olympus, rulers of the cosmos — each sovereign over their sacred domain.

⚡

Zeus

King of the Gods

I

Supreme ruler of Olympus, god of sky, thunder, and justice. Wielder of the divine thunderbolt, Zeus governs the heavens and holds dominion over gods and mortals alike, enforcing the sacred laws of hospitality.

Symbols: Eagle · Thunderbolt · Oak
🔱

Poseidon

God of the Sea

II

Lord of the deep, earth-shaker, tamer of horses. Poseidon rules the oceans and their depths, commanding storms and earthquakes with his fearsome trident, brother to Zeus and rival to Athena for Athens.

Symbols: Trident · Horse · Dolphin
💀

Hades

God of the Underworld

III

Silent king of the dead, guardian of all souls who pass beyond the mortal veil. Neither evil nor cruel, Hades rules the realm below with impartiality — he who receives all equally in the end.

Symbols: Helm · Cerberus · Cypress
🦉

Athena

Goddess of Wisdom

IV

Sprung fully armored from the forehead of Zeus, goddess of wisdom, warfare strategy, and crafts. Patron of Athens and protectress of heroes, Athena favors intellect and cunning over brute force.

Symbols: Owl · Aegis · Olive Tree
⚔️

Ares

God of War

V

The fearsome god of war and bloodlust, embodying the brutal and violent aspects of battle. Unlike Athena's strategic warfare, Ares revels in carnage itself — unpredictable, passionate, and terrible in his fury.

Symbols: Spear · Shield · Vulture
🌹

Aphrodite

Goddess of Love

VI

Born from the sea-foam, goddess of love, beauty, desire, and pleasure. Her power transcends all others — even the mightiest gods bow to Eros's golden arrows at her command. The face that launched a thousand ships.

Symbols: Dove · Rose · Myrtle

Chronicles of Olympus

Sacred Myths

Tales woven at the dawn of time — passed from poet to priest, from Muse to mortal.

Chronicle I · In the Beginning

The Creation of All Things

Before all things existed, there was only Chaos — a dark, formless void of infinite potential. From this primordial emptiness emerged Gaia, the Earth, Tartarus, the deep abyss, and Eros, the force of love that would bind creation together.

Gaia brought forth Uranus, the starry heavens, to be both her equal and her mate. Together they conceived the twelve Titans, the three Cyclopes, and the three Hecatoncheires — beings of terrifying power with a hundred hands each.

Uranus, fearful of his monstrous offspring, cast them into Tartarus. Gaia, wounded and enraged, fashioned a great sickle and implored her children to act. Only Cronus, the youngest Titan, dared answer her call — setting in motion the first great revolution of the cosmos.

Source: Hesiod · Theogony, c. 700 BCE

Chronicle II · The War of Ages

The Titanomachy

For ten terrible years, the universe was torn asunder by war. The Olympians — children of Cronus, who had swallowed them at birth to prevent a prophecy of his own overthrow — battled the Titans across the heavens and earth.

Zeus, having freed his siblings from Cronus's belly and liberated the Cyclopes from their prison, wielded the thunderbolt for the first time. Poseidon bore the trident, and Hades donned the helm of invisibility. The very mountains shook and seas boiled.

When at last the Titans fell, Zeus cast them into the depths of Tartarus, behind bronze walls, where even the light of day could never reach. A new age began — the age of the Olympians — and the cosmos took the shape we know.

Source: Hesiod · Theogony · Books IV–V

Chronicle III · The Long Journey Home

The Odyssey

Ten years after the fall of Troy, Odysseus of Ithaca had not yet returned home. Cursed by Poseidon for blinding his son Polyphemus the Cyclops, he wandered the wine-dark sea — encountering monsters, sorcerers, and gods at every turn.

He passed through the island of Circe, who turned men to swine; descended living into the realm of Hades; sailed between Scylla and Charybdis; and resisted the song of the Sirens, lashed to his mast as his crew rowed with wax-stopped ears.

Guided always by Athena's unseen hand, Odysseus finally reached Ithaca's rocky shore, where faithful Penelope waited at her loom, unraveling by night what she wove by day — an act of love that outlasted twenty years of war and wandering.

Source: Homer · The Odyssey, c. 800 BCE

Chronicle IV · The Theft of Fire

The Bound Titan

Prometheus, the Forethought, was among the wisest of the Titans and a friend to humankind. When Zeus withheld fire from mortals — condemning them to cold, darkness, and ignorance — Prometheus climbed to the forge of Hephaestus and stole a burning ember, concealed in a hollow fennel stalk.

Civilization was born in that stolen flame. Humans learned to cook, to forge metal, to build and worship. But Zeus's fury was boundless. He ordered Prometheus chained to a crag in the Caucasus Mountains, where each day an eagle would devour his immortal liver — and each night it would grow back.

For thirty thousand years he endured, until Heracles at last shattered his chains. Prometheus, unbowed, had kept his secret: he knew the fate of Zeus, should the king of gods pursue a certain sea-nymph. He chose suffering over surrender — the first martyr of the human cause.

Source: Aeschylus · Prometheus Bound, c. 460 BCE
